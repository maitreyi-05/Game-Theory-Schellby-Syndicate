The Original Theory — Ellsberg 1968
The Art of Blackmail · Daniel Ellsberg, RAND 1968

In The Theory and Practice of Blackmail, Daniel Ellsberg — later famous for the Pentagon Papers — formalised coercion as a strategic game. His central argument was deceptively simple: a threat is only as powerful as its credibility, and most threats are structurally incredible.

The entire art of blackmail lies in making an irrational action appear rational. The blackmailer must convince the victim that punishment will follow non-compliance — even though, rationally, carrying out the punishment is costly and gains nothing once the bluff has been called.

— Daniel Ellsberg, RAND Paper P-3883, 1968

Ellsberg built on Thomas Schelling's earlier work on bargaining and commitment to introduce the concept of critical risk: the minimum probability of punishment that a victim must believe exists before they will comply. He showed that:

The three-part structure of blackmail

1. The demand. The blackmailer (Iran) announces a threat: comply with my demand, or face punishment. This establishes the coercive claim.

2. The credibility problem. The victim (USA) knows that carrying out the punishment is costly for the blackmailer too. A rational blackmailer would prefer not to follow through. So why believe the threat?

3. The critical risk. USA does not need to be certain Iran will follow through — only sufficiently uncertain. If USA assigns probability p > p* to Iran carrying out the punishment, the expected cost of resisting exceeds the cost of complying. Blackmail works on probability, not certainty.

Ellsberg's four credibility mechanisms

Commitment devices — Delegating the escalation decision to an agent who cannot back down (e.g. a military commander with independent authority) removes the Back Down option entirely, making the threat credible by construction.

Reputational stakes — Publicly staking national honour on following through makes backing down more costly than the punishment itself. Iran's rhetorical commitment to its nuclear programme operates exactly this way.

Uncertainty exploitation — If the victim is genuinely unsure whether the blackmailer's Back Down payoff is positive or negative, the threat gains credibility from ambiguity alone. This is the formal basis of the Bayesian extension in Tab 05.

Apparent irrationality — An actor who appears unpredictable or ideologically driven cannot be modelled as a rational agent, so the victim cannot infer that Back Down is preferred. Perceived irrationality is a strategic asset.

Why this applies to USA–Iran

Iran's nuclear programme, its proxy network (Hezbollah, Houthis, Iraqi militias), and its revolutionary ideological framing are all instruments that raise USA's perceived probability p that Iran is the Hard type — willing to carry out punishment even at cost to itself. The formal model in this simulator derives exactly when those instruments succeed in pushing p past the critical risk threshold p* = (3+B)/(4+B), and what role China-Russia backing plays in that calculation.

Intel Assessment — Incomplete Information

Type H — Hard Iran

Ideologically committed. Backing down is humiliating — payoff −k (k > 0). So Type H always carries out its threat if USA resists. Dominant strategy: Carry Out.

Type S — Soft Iran

Rational and cost-conscious. Back Down yields 1, Carry Out yields 0. So Type S always backs down if USA resists. This is the SPE type. Dominant strategy: Back Down.

USA's Expected Payoff from Resist
E[Resist] = p(−B) + (1−p)(4) = 4 − p(4 + B)

USA complies when E[Resist] < Comply, i.e. when p > (3+B)/(4+B) = p*.

where p* = (3 + B) / (4 + B). When Iran threatens in equilibrium, both types send the same threat — a pooling equilibrium. Bayes' rule cannot update USA's beliefs, so beliefs remain at prior p. Off-path (Case A), PBE is supported by any off-path belief p' < p*.

Key Intel
  • B increases p*, raising the credibility bar. p* = (3+B)/(4+B) is strictly increasing in B. Higher rival bloc support raises the threshold USA needs before complying — Iran must project more resolve as B grows, not less.
  • Two joint conditions required. Blackmail succeeds only when p > p* (credibility) AND B > a−1 (incentive). Meeting one alone is not enough.
  • a and B play structurally different roles. B sets the credibility threshold p*. a sets Iran's incentive to threaten at all. They operate through separate channels.
  • SPE is the p = 0 special case. When USA is certain Iran is Soft (p = 0), we recover the perfect-information cooperation result exactly.
  • Off-path beliefs (Case A). When both types restrain, a threat is off the equilibrium path. The PBE is supported by any off-path belief p' < p* — Bayes' rule leaves these unconstrained.
  • Real-world credibility tools. Iran's nuclear programme, proxy networks, and ideological framing raise USA's posterior belief p toward p*. They are the formal equivalent of Ellsberg's credibility mechanisms.
